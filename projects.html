INTERVENTIONS | Spring 2020

Environmental racism, food injustice, and waste colonialism are all systematic ways in which the marginalized are oppressed. By taking advantage of their inability to fight back, those with can further the gap between themselves and those without. While the systems and policies that produce these conditions vary, the points of intervention can often overlap at a protest.

From its initial conception, this project was meant to create an experience so that victims of environmental injustices can ground those with power into their realities. Walking in someone’s footsteps has a stronger impact than simply communicating statistics, which can feel more abstract. The common thread between all of the group members’ concepts was a desire to protect and empower those who were pushing for change.

The goal became clear: we wanted to design a way of conducting nonviolent protest, utilizing the audience’s sense and accessible materials. Additionally, it was realized that an interactive and memorable experience could be created through art.

The aesthetics of these artifacts are designed so that they do not initially arouse suspicion, but are engaging and attention-grabbing. Upon interacting with the objects, the target should be able to discover hidden notes or memos describing its relevance and the source of pollution it’s meant to raise awareness for. This model of interaction was inspired by the Trojan Horse.

Themes

  • Awareness of Consequences
  • Distance/Experience
  • Protection
  • The Most Affected are those with the Least Power

Human Factors Engineering @ Johnson & Johnson | Fall 2019

Human Factors Engineering (HFE) is a discipline that aims to improve ease of use and mitigate risk of user error, particularly in the nuclear, aerospace, and medical industries.

During the duration of my Arch Away semester, I had the opportunity to work for DePuy Synthes, one of the many companies under the Johnson & Johnson umbrella. As one of the largest manufacturers of orthopedic implants and neurosurgery products, DePuy Synthes has ongoing projects in trauma and spine, which I was able to support as a co-op.

I worked within the Industrial Design and Human Factors (IDHF) department, which leads contextual research to discover unmet user needs, creates usability specifications and requirements, and iteratively tests with stakeholders throughout a robust formative and summative usability process. The team also designs and evaluates user interfaces (hardware and software) for medical devices.

Cool stuff I got to do:

  • Conducted Use-Related Risk Analysis for orthopedic/trauma products.
  • Ensured products aligned with best practice for human-centered design principles.
  • Produced FDA-compliant user testing protocol and documentation.
  • Developed design recommendations from user insights.

AGRIculture | Summer 2019

This project aimed to design a lightweight and affordable hydroponics system for use in local communities, in a way that address the socioeconomic inequalities inherent in food deserts so that the large-scale harms of industrialized agriculture can also be minimized.

In terms of the pre-existing approaches in this problem space, most hydroponic systems are extremely cost-ineffective and rarely accommodate subsistence crops. Ethnographic research with the Sanctuary for Independent Media had also established that certain methods of employing hydroponic technology are unsuccessful and will misalign with the needs of the community. Thus, the goals of this proposal aim to fill this unmet niche.

Opening the Black Box of Gendered Restrooms | Spring 2019

This project, mainly meant to be more of a provocative social science/art project, was created as part of Prof. Schaffer's "Gender, Science, and Technology" class. At the time, there was a great deal of discussion in both the political and social spheres regarding gender-neutral bathrooms and transgender rights.

In the design itself, all users enter from one entrance into an antechamber. They filter into individual spaces, each equipped with a toilet, sink, trash bin, menstrual supplies, and baby changing station. They can additionally choose to exit through a separate door, avoiding interaction with others in the public bathroom.

The following presentation delves into the sociology behind gender and public discomforts around restrooms. Additionally, there are floorplans for a proposed design of a more accessible bathroom, which current gendered restrooms can be retrofitted into.

The Easy-Slice | Fall 2018

This product is an educational tool primarily designed for students in culinary classes. It consists of a cutting board and a chef's knife with a specialized handle that encourages proper knife-grip and slicing technique through muscle memory.

Both the knife and handle were redesigned to meet the needs of our users. The handle, in particular, is meant to be more comfortable than traditional knife handles. However, it will feel uncomfortable if the user holds the knife with an improper grip. This final design was created through observation as well as interviews with culinary experts and educators.

Additional user groups explored were those with physical impairments and disabilities, as the constrained motion of the blade allows these individuals to more safely control a knife.

Historical Design Series | Fall 2018

Operating off a design brief, I was constrained with the task of emulating the style of prominent ceramics artist Eva Zeisel. This model was entirely created from a series of sketches and ultimately modeled in a CAD program.

Comprised of a ceramic body and a glass base, this coffee pot invokes Zeisel's elegant design while incorporating pour-over components for brewing freshly ground coffee beans.

An Analysis of Accessibility in NYC's Public Transit | Fall 2017

An analysis of the design and efficacy of the New York City transit system in serving the needs of those with physical disabilities. Using data and reports from the MTA, censuses, and previous studies, I compared the city's transit system with others of the same time period, as well as developments that have been made since then. This report also includes and analysis of current accomodations (such as ramps and elevators) and reccomendations for integrating these.
