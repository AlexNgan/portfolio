February 16, 2018

Approaches to Design: Form vs. Function in Mordernism

Looking at different designers, I first gravitated to IKEA as a design firm. Their lead designer, Marcus Engman, seems to push for what the company calls "democratic design" and the company as a whole pursues a Modernist aesthetic. That approach hinges upon the philosophy that form follows function.

Yet, as we began discussions on the movement in the Design, Culture, and Society (DCS) course, the issues of creating a universal solution has a huge fault - it assumes that everyone's needs are the same and can be addressed in the same manner. So, is a "universal" design even possible?

February 13, 2018

The Evaluation Matrix

The matrix approach introduced to us, by which we'll hope to evaluate the efficacy and success of our project, is heavily rooted in engineering. No doubt about it. The approach is one that I've seen while on my robotics team and it's certainly one I've seen used by professional engineers. It's heavily regimented and data-driven, but since you're the one who defines the matrix, I can see that the evaluation of success can be very one-sided.

Success is not universal.

What works for one person may not work for another. Yet, it can be more challenging to proceed without any decision matrix. And I guess some part of design means acknowledging that you cannot accomodate everyone.

February 8, 2018

Redefining Relationships

In the work of Natalie Jerimijenko, we see that design can redefine existing relationships, challenging them, to provoke thought. I was intrigued by her project on the NYC High Line, an old aboveground subway track that has since been repurposed as one of the city's nicest parks; the space has already been redefined once.

Jerimijenko intends to redefine it a second time, with her OOZ project, to change the social landscape of NYC again. And, in the project we're pursuing in Studio II, this phenomena can be seen at play as well. Fascinating.

February 1, 2018

Designing a Design Process

In PDI II, as my group moved past the problem definition, we noted that we had to outline the design process that we'd need to use moving into Unit 2. I don't know about you, but for me, there feels to be something counterintuitive to creating a rigid schedule for something as fluid and malleable as design and creativity.

At the same time, I know for my experience on a robotics team (a discpline firmly rooted in engineering), a strict process and series of deadlines way the key to success. The process we used was as follows:

Analysis → Goals → Design → Evaluation

This entire process would iterate through. Analysis of the issue would determine our goals, which would be assembled into a priority list. Designs and protoypes would be generated in response and those would be evaluated. How well does this one accomplish the goal we set forth? Is this goal even the most important priority, given our design constraints? This approach as a whole was very scientific and every choice had to be backed up with data or evidence.

Striking a balance is what we should aim for, I suppose. That's going to be a recurring theme for PDI, isn't it?

January 23, 2018

At the Cross-Roads of Art and Engineering

Engineering is as much of an art as well, art. At least in my eyes. They both hold great value as individual disciplines yet, they're often pitted against each other. This week's reading, chapter one of Nigel Cross' Engineering Design Methods: Strategies for Product Design, brought an interesting thought to my mind.

The sum is greater than either of its parts.

That is, the intersection of engineering and art is where both are strongest.

Cross writes the following: "A potter will make a pot by working directly with the clay, and without first making any sketches or drawings of the pot" (pg. 4).

My mother, who pursues pottery as a hobby, is an engineer. She solves complex issues extremely methodically and in many ways, applies concepts introduced in the PDI curriculum without realizing. Her interdsciplinary approach makes her a very adept problem solver at work and an amazing artist. She designs her pieces, counter to Cross' statement. She measures out the amount of clay. She sketches and sketches. Her pieces are often more complex, beautiful, and creative than those of her peers, who are only thinking as artists.

In the same vein, this is why I think the PDI appoach is so powerful. The issues we aim to tackle are so broad that they cannot be solved by one disclipine alone.

January 19, 2018

A Design Pitfall: The Sunk-Cost Fallacy

I'd like to note first that I am a mentor for two robotics teams that compete in the FIRST Robotics Competition. Earlier this week, I had the pleasure of observing, for myself, one of the first ideas introduced in PDI Studio II.

The fact that no one, no one AT ALL wants to let go of their baby.

One of them, determined early on that they'd need a arm of some sort to grab a game piece. And that the arm would have to have a wrist. And be mounted to a lift. There was no easy way to do this, and a few ideas were proposed, nearly all of them involving dozens of parts and quite a few actuations. Math and basic designs were drawn up for all of them.

A few days later, another design was proposed. It would feature one less major articulation, yet accomplish the same tasks. This design was all positives: less material, lower cost, more durability, and a structure that was more easily manufactured. You'd think everyone would jump right onboard, right? Nope.

Now, back to the Sunk-Cost Fallacy, the idea that something feels valuable to you because of an emotional or financial investment; it's hard to give up on a design that you've sunk time into. I saw that here, even with half-baked designs, people were hesitant to give up their ideas and short-term progress; it was a real lesson in team dynamics and our mentalities as human designers.

Passion, a strong driving force, can also be an Achilles' heel. The same motivations that propel us forwards can also leave us clinging to the past. Just something we have to keep in mind as designers.
