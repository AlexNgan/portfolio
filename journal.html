April 19, 2018

On Afrofuturism and the contributions of African Americans

Afrofuturism, a cultural aesthetic built on the intersection with the African diaspora and technology, has been brought into the mainstream lens by media such as Marvel's Black Panther.

Lupita Nyong'o describes the idea beautifully in an interview following the release of Black Panther: "What coloniasm did was that it rewrote our history, it changed our narrative. Our global narrative is one of poverty and strife. The wealth of the continent is seldom seen on such a global scale...It's a reimagining of what could have been possible, had Africa been allowed to realize itself, for itself."

The genre of Afrofuturism is a type of sci-fi in which the world is imagined as a utopia and future shaped by black innovation. But, while afrofuturism currently occupies a more "fictional" space, there has to be the acknowledgement that a huge part of the world we live in is made possible by the innovations of African Americans. Lewis Latimer created the filament that made lightbulbs affordable for household use. Garrett Morgan invented the traffic lights that run our cities. The automatic gear shift is attributed to Richard Spikes. Dr. Patricia E. Bath pioneered a laser surgery for treating cataracts. Frederick McKinley Jones developed refrigerated trucks. George Alcorn built x-ray spectrometers to be used by NASA. In that sense, afrofuturism is already "real".

April 11, 2018

A Truly Universal Design?

Building off a previous entry (see Feb. 16), I wanted to focus, once more, on universal design.

Currently, in PDI Studio II, I'm working on a project that makes sex ed more accessible, that is - we intend to make the material more easily found and understandable. We first turned to the Internet: could we build a solution online that serves as a library of sorts. Then we realized that for students with visual disabilities (who already are poorly served by these classes in traditional media), this would potentially make the information (particularly the 3D biological models we hoped to develop) even harder to access.

This begs the question: is there a platform or medium that is universally accesible?

In PDI Studio I, I had researched the lack of physical accessibility within the NYC public transportation system. Initially, I had assumed that a solution for those with the greatest physical disability would be a solution across the board, like a universal design. Essentially, I thought that installing ramps, for example, would mean everyone would be guaranteed accessibility. However, after discussing this a bit with Dr. Raquel Velho, I learned this was not the case. Ramps, while they provide accessibility to wheelchair users, can be difficult to use for the elderly, particularly those with walkers.

And yes, if your next question was if I had considered elevators, I did. (They already exist in the NYC subways system, but are broken most of the time because of high usage and repair costs.)

Back to Studio II. The short answer is no, I don't think there's ever a truly universal design. But I also don't believe that's a bad thing. If a solution was targetted and meant for one very specific type of user, that wouldn't make it any less valid, especially if this group hasn't been well served by conventional design. By using different approaches and tending away from generalization, we can learn to be better designers.

April 9, 2018

Adapting Design to Human Behavior

I recently followed one of my friends, an forth-year architecture student, around her studio one afternoon and I was absolutely fascinated with one of the projects within that class. They were trying to design and build livable structures (mainly meant to be used as emergency shelters) out of plastic bottles that would otherwise be thrown away. These bottles, engineered with a special interlocking shape, slot together like bricks.

I found it quite ingenious - certainly, building material could be made by recycling bottles, but that takes a lot of time and energy (from collecting and processing the plastic bottles) so using the bottles as they are can be quite efficient. The bottles could be filled with a material like sand to make them solid. The plastic means structures built with them would be waterproof.

This experience reminded me of a lesson in PDI Studio I. It's a lot easier to use human behavior and utilize it in design than it is to try and force a change in behavior via design. People just won't use something unintuitive and difficult, simple as that. But, if you take note of what people do naturally and build off of that, both the design and its relationship to users can be strengthened. The example brought up in Studio I was Bank of America's "Keep the Change" program. Researchers noted that, when paying in cash, people tended to drop the change into savings jars. When they began using credit cards and the like, this savings technique was gone and people began saving money a bit less. In response, Bank of America developed the program so purchases are rounded up to the nearest dollar, so the change is then shifted to one's savings account - and customers loved it.

Anyways, back to the bottles. As I began to look into the concept, I also noted that similar attempts had been made elsewhere. Harvard has the Polli-Brick. A small company launched Botbrick last year. Perhaps these designs will break into the mainstream one day.

March 22, 2018

Technology as an Amplfier

In one of the other classes I've been taking (Design, Culture, and Society with Prof. Langdon Winner) we've spent the semester looking at the cyclical relationship between technology and society. In doing so, I've started to notice a trend.

On the macro scale, new technologies aren't creating new issues. Instead, they make flaws within systems (and human nature!) more apparent.

In the case of the Internet, which was once designed as something to facilitate transfer of information and knowledge, a lot of the conversations and issues that now surround it hinge upon the issues it has brought to light. Yet, these issues have always been there, just less apparent. Now, with social media, and a daily (if not hourly) news cycle, we are innundated with an unprecedented amount of information. We can no longer ignore these problems.

People always laud technology and industrialization as an incontrovertible good - these things are improvements to our lives. I feel that this stance ignores the other possibility inherent within the introduction of technology; design as a whole is a force for change and the changes it enables aren't always for the best.

March 15, 2018

The Uncomfortable

During spring break, I came across a designer who uses the craft to do something I initially found ridiculous and counter productive. After I moved past the knee-jerk reaction and allowed myself to reflect on her work, I came to an interesting realization.

Architect Katerina Kamprani's The Uncomfortable is a series of designs intended to provoke. Her projects are items that not only serve no purpose, but instead, make it difficult or even impossible for the user to interface with at all. Ranging from a salt shaker that would only release one grain at a time to chairs that cannot be used, the project presents a certain type of genius.

In order for Kamprani to make something Uncomfortable, she first must understand the qualities of the original artifact that makes it desirable in the first place, then completely turn those qualities on their heads. In the case of the Uncomfortable Chairs, all the things we, as users, look for in a good chair (comfort, attractiveness, etc.) are subverted. The Uncomfortable Chairs are made of a hard plastic and odd-looking - everything about the design is uncomfortable, from it's physical form to the way our psyche perceives the objects. All of the artifacts within this project trigger some reaction of confusion or harmless discomfort.

I found this incredibly fascinating because we, as designers and engineers, are trained to move in the complete opposite direction. In practices such as decision matrices and strengths analysis, we're always looking for ways to improve (although what can be considered as such is quite subjective).

The Evaluation Matrix

The matrix approach introduced to us, by which we'll hope to evaluate the efficacy and success of our project, is heavily rooted in engineering. No doubt about it. The approach is one that I've seen while on my robotics team and it's certainly one I've seen used by professional engineers. It's heavily regimented and data-driven, but since you're the one who defines the matrix, I can see that the evaluation of success can be very one-sided.

Success is not universal.

What works for one person may not work for another. Yet, it can be more challenging to proceed without any decision matrix. And I guess some part of design means acknowledging that you cannot accomodate everyone.

February 1, 2018

Designing a Design Process

In PDI II, as my group moved past the problem definition, we noted that we had to outline the design process that we'd need to use moving into Unit 2. I don't know about you, but for me, there feels to be something counterintuitive to creating a rigid schedule for something as fluid and malleable as design and creativity.

At the same time, I know for my experience on a robotics team (a discpline firmly rooted in engineering), a strict process and series of deadlines way the key to success. The process we used was as follows:

Analysis → Goals → Design → Evaluation

This entire process would iterate through. Analysis of the issue would determine our goals, which would be assembled into a priority list. Designs and protoypes would be generated in response and those would be evaluated. How well does this one accomplish the goal we set forth? Is this goal even the most important priority, given our design constraints? This approach as a whole was very scientific and every choice had to be backed up with data or evidence.

Striking a balance is what we should aim for, I suppose. That's going to be a recurring theme for PDI, isn't it?

January 23, 2018

At the Cross-Roads of Art and Engineering

Engineering is as much of an art as well, art. At least in my eyes. They both hold great value as individual disciplines yet, they're often pitted against each other. This week's reading, chapter one of Nigel Cross' Engineering Design Methods: Strategies for Product Design, brought an interesting thought to my mind.

The sum is greater than either of its parts.

That is, the intersection of engineering and art is where both are strongest.

Cross writes the following: "A potter will make a pot by working directly with the clay, and without first making any sketches or drawings of the pot" (pg. 4).

My mother, who pursues pottery as a hobby, is an engineer. She solves complex issues extremely methodically and in many ways, applies concepts introduced in the PDI curriculum without realizing. Her interdsciplinary approach makes her a very adept problem solver at work and an amazing artist. She designs her pieces, counter to Cross' statement. She measures out the amount of clay. She sketches and sketches. Her pieces are often more complex, beautiful, and creative than those of her peers, who are only thinking as artists.

In the same vein, this is why I think the PDI appoach is so powerful. The issues we aim to tackle are so broad that they cannot be solved by one disclipine alone.

January 19, 2018

A Design Pitfall: The Sunk-Cost Fallacy

I'd like to note first that I am a mentor for two robotics teams that compete in the FIRST Robotics Competition. Every year, a new challenge is introduced and 2018's can be found here.

Earlier this week, the season kicked off and I had the pleasure of observing, for myself, one of the first ideas introduced in PDI Studio II.

The fact that no one, no one AT ALL wants to let go of their baby.

One of the teams, determined early on that they'd need a arm of some sort to grab a game piece. And that the arm would have to have a wrist. And be mounted to a lift. There was no easy way to do this, and a few ideas were proposed, nearly all of them involving dozens of parts and quite a few actuations. Math and basic designs were drawn up for all of them.

A few days later, another design was proposed. It would feature one less major articulation, yet accomplish the same tasks. This design was all positives: less material, lower cost, more durability, and a structure that was more easily manufactured. You'd think everyone would jump right onboard, right? Nope.

Now, back to the Sunk-Cost Fallacy, the idea that something feels valuable to you because of an emotional or financial investment; it's hard to give up on a design that you've sunk time into. I saw that here, even with half-baked designs, people were hesitant to give up their ideas and short-term progress; it was a real lesson in team dynamics and our mentalities as human designers.

Passion, a strong driving force, can also be an Achilles' heel. The same motivations that propel us forwards can also leave us clinging to the past. Just something we have to keep in mind as designers.
